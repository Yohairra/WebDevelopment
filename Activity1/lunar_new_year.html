Description

Lunar New Year, also known as Spring Festival or Chinese New Year, stands as one of the world's most vibrant and widely celebrated cultural holidays. While primarily associated with China, this festival is observed throughout East Asia and in communities across the globe, making it a truly international celebration that transcends borders.

Dating back over 3,500 years to the Shang Dynasty in China, Lunar New Year began as a celebration marking the end of winter and the beginning of spring. The lunar calendar determines its date, typically falling between late January and mid-February on the Gregorian calendar. Each year is associated with one of twelve zodiac animals—Rat, Ox, Tiger, Rabbit, Dragon, Snake, Horse, Goat, Monkey, Rooster, Dog, and Pig—creating a repeating twelve-year cycle that influences traditions and customs.

  • Red Decorations: Symbolizes good fortune and joy
  • Lucky Money: Elders give ang pao containing money to children, symbolizing good wishes and blessings
  • Performances: Lion and dragon dances, as well as, firecrackers and fireworks to drive away evil spirits
